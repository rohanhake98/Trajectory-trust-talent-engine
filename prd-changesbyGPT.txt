## 1. Problem Statement

This is not a resume matching problem.

The dataset contains 100,000 candidates, only a small number of literal title matches, 23 behavioral signals, and explicit trap patterns such as keyword stuffing and synthetic profiles. The real task is to identify candidates who are most likely to become a strong hire, not just those who look strong on paper.

### What the dataset is really measuring
It measures whether a system can combine:
- current fit,
- career trajectory,
- behavioral signals,
- and trust / authenticity signals

to rank candidates for a specific senior AI engineering role.

### What the dataset is NOT measuring
It does not directly measure:
- actual coding ability,
- long-term job performance,
- personality,
- team fit,
- or whether a candidate is universally “best.”

The challenge is about ranking under uncertainty, not proving absolute quality.

**One-line pitch:**  
*We rank candidates by where they are going and whether we can trust the map, not by where they already are.*

---

## 2. Why the Obvious Solutions Fail

Most teams will build one of these:
- candidate-job matching
- resume ranking
- skill similarity search
- recommendation systems
- ATS-style scoring
- title classifiers
- embedding-based search
- keyword overlap ranking

These are too shallow because they miss:
- trajectory,
- hidden learning,
- recruiter convertibility,
- and fake / misleading profiles.

This PRD intentionally avoids those trivial solutions.

## 3. Product Idea

### Core idea
Build a **talent intelligence engine** that finds:

1. candidates who are growing into the target role,
2. candidates who are realistically hireable,
3. candidates who are not honeypots or keyword-stuffed traps.

### Key principle
Do not rank the “best-looking resume.”  
Rank the “best real hire.”

### Simple definition
A candidate should rank high only if:
- their skills are relevant,
- their career is moving in the right direction,
- their behavior suggests they will respond and convert,
- and their profile is trustworthy.

## 8. Proposed Solution

### Main concept
Use four scoring layers:

1. **Static Fit**  
   Basic relevance to the role.

2. **Trajectory Score**  
   Whether the candidate is moving toward the role.

3. **Convertibility Score**  
   Whether the candidate is likely to reply, interview, and accept.

4. **Trust Score**  
   Whether the profile looks real and internally consistent.

### Business outcome
The system surfaces:
- underrated candidates,
- realistic hires,
- and suspicious profiles.

## 9. Feature Design

### A. Static Fit Features
- title-to-role similarity
- skill overlap with target role
- weighted skill proficiency
- endorsements
- years of experience
- education tier

### B. Trajectory Features
- career velocity
- title progression direction
- movement toward AI / ML / senior engineering
- skill acquisition rate
- time-to-role estimate
- transition relevance across jobs

### C. Trust / Anomaly Features
- overlapping career dates
- impossible timelines
- too many skills with low depth
- suspiciously perfect title-match profiles
- unrealistic certificate / experience combinations
- claimed seniority mismatch

### D. Behavioral / Convertibility Features
- last active
- open to work
- recruiter response rate
- response speed
- interview completion rate
- offer acceptance rate
- notice period
- relocation willingness
- work mode match
- salary fit

## 10. Core Model Idea

This is not a deep learning competition.

Use a practical, explainable pipeline:
- feature engineering,
- rule-based anomaly checks,
- graph-based transition features,
- calibrated ranking.

### Graph idea
Treat careers, titles, companies, and skills as a graph:
- candidate nodes
- skill nodes
- title nodes
- company nodes
- industry nodes

Use career transitions from the whole population to estimate:
- how many realistic hops away a candidate is from the target role,
- and which profiles are actually on a believable path.

This is the “between the data” layer.

## 11. Scoring Formula

### Final score
```text
final_score = TrustScore × (0.25 × StaticFit + 0.40 × TrajectoryScore + 0.35 × ConvertibilityScore)
````

### Sub-scores

```text
StaticFit = 0.5 × title_skill_similarity
          + 0.3 × skill_overlap_weighted
          + 0.2 × education_experience_band

TrajectoryScore = 0.35 × career_velocity
                + 0.25 × transition_relevance
                + 0.20 × skill_acquisition_rate
                + 0.20 × (1 - normalized_time_to_role_estimate)

ConvertibilityScore = 0.20 × recency_factor
                    + 0.15 × open_to_work
                    + 0.15 × recruiter_response_rate
                    + 0.15 × interview_completion_rate
                    + 0.15 × offer_acceptance_rate
                    + 0.10 × notice_period_factor
                    + 0.10 × work_mode_and_relocation_match

TrustScore = 1 - clip(0.4 × timeline_inconsistency
                    + 0.3 × skill_stuffing_ratio
                    + 0.3 × synthetic_pattern_score, 0, 1)
```

### Important rule

Trust is multiplicative, not just additive.
That means fake profiles get pushed down hard.

## 12. Reasoning Text Rule

Every candidate in the final output must have a short explanation.

### The explanation must:

* use only extracted facts,
* be template-based,
* mention the top 2–3 strongest signals,
* and never invent details.

### Example format

* “Strong AI-adjacent progression, high recruiter response rate, and recent activity suggest strong hiring potential.”
* “Exact title match, but weak trajectory and low behavioral engagement reduce confidence.”
* “Profile shows suspiciously broad skills with weak depth, so trust is low.”

---

## 13. Output Format

### Columns

* tie-break by `candidate_id`

## 18. Risks and Mitigations

### Risk: too much focus on title match

Mitigation: give more weight to trajectory and convertibility.

### Risk: keyword-stuffed fake profiles rank too high

Mitigation: make trust score multiplicative.

### Risk: explanations become vague or hallucinated

Mitigation: use templates from extracted features only.

### Risk: pipeline becomes too slow

Mitigation: keep everything CPU-only and avoid large models.

### Risk: graph layer adds complexity without value

Mitigation: keep it lightweight and fallback-safe.
